What this document is, and how to read it

Overview for DK

This is a paper-fidelity wireframe of the Week-1 journey for COGS 160 Fall students. Each section sketches one surface (page or panel) of the journey, with annotations on the layout decisions, the data each surface depends on, and the upstream work needed to build it. The wireframe is intentionally low-fidelity: the visual styling is provisional, the content is placeholder where placeholders are honest, and the interactions are described rather than implemented. Nothing here is wired to the real corpus; the point is to make the journey shape reviewable in one sitting before any backend work begins.

Three colour codes are used throughout. Cream-and-teal blocks are mock UI elements (what the student sees). Italic blocks in cream are annotations explaining design decisions. Amber-tinted blocks marked Depends on flag the upstream work the surface needs (a backend, a content page, an agent, a panel). Purple-tinted blocks marked Decision flag the choices the student makes inside the surface.

The eight surfaces are connected by internal links; click any surface name in the navigation bar above, or click any inline reference to another surface, to jump. The journey shape itself is sketched in surface 1; surfaces 2–9 are the destinations the student reaches as Week 1 unfolds.

Updated timeline per DK 2026-05-17. Week 1 finds a topic. Weeks 2–6 design the experiment (extended from earlier "Week 5" deadline). Weeks 7–8 implement. Week 9 pilot among classmates. Week 9–10 first pilot subject (from class). Week 10 write up. Week 11 submit an APA-conforming psych paper. Students should be building throughout the quarter so the paper is almost done by Week 10. The paper must show one single result; predictions must be in chart form.

Annotations

The week indicator is the most important orienting cue. The system knows which week of the course the student is in (from calendar integration); the student should not have to ask. If the week indicator is misaligned (student is in Week 1 but sees Week 1 indicator and they think they are in Week 2), that itself is a fixable bug.
The phase strip showing weeks 1–11 with the current week highlighted is a Track-4-style waypoint affordance: at every moment the student can answer "where am I, how did I get here, what happens next." The phase strip is repeated on every Week-N landing.
The three CTAs are entry points to three different working modes for the same Week-1 goal. The student picks one. They can return to the landing and try another mode if the first does not land. The fourth box ("Recommended supporting reading") is reading, not action — it is the orientation material the student should consume before or during the topic-finding work.
The "By end of Week 1 you should have" panel is the Track-4 exit-condition affordance: an explicit success-criteria checklist. When the student arrives at Surface 9 (deliverable capture) they will be asked to confirm each item.

Dependencies

The week-aware routing requires the system to know which week of the COGS 160 Fall course is current and which week each student should be on. Calendar integration plus per-student progress tracking. Not yet wired.
The phase strip with eleven weeks needs the canonical Fall course phase list. fall160_schedule.html has it. The strip should pull from that source rather than hard-coding.

Decisions

Should the three CTAs be ordered DYK → Evaluate-Paper → VR-explainer (as drawn), or should VR-explainer come first because a student who has not read it cannot use the other two effectively? CW recommends DYK first (most engaging entry); DK to confirm.

Surface 4 · Evaluate a paper for my VR experiment (admit-mode · Week 1)

Reached from Surface 1 via "Evaluate a paper for my VR experiment" · file: ka_evaluate_paper_for_vr.html (new)
The substitution skill is invoked twice in the COGS 160 Fall pipeline: first in admit-mode (this surface, Week 1) — does a VR-tractable substitute exist for the paper's outcome measure, yes or no? — and again in choice-mode (Surface 4b, Week 3) — given that several admissible measures exist, which one should the student pick, and what are the trade-offs? The two modes share the same knowledge base but expose different affordances. The clarification follows DK's 2026-05-18 comment that pruning and choice are distinct stages; the full argument is in docs/USER_JOURNEY_COMMENTS_RESPONSE_2026-05-18.md §5.

What this surface is for (in response to DK's question)

The use case: a student arrives having read a paper (in a course earlier, on their own, in a coffee shop) and wants to know whether they can build their VR experiment around it. The system answers four questions. Are the paper's outcome measures VR-tractable as-is? If not, what substitute measures could probe the same construct? Do other papers in the corpus already test something similar in VR? Does the topic survive the 7-to-10-week project constraint? The surface is the entry point for students who already have a paper in mind; Surfaces 2 and 3 are the entry points for students who do not. This surface answers admit/reject; Surface 4b answers ranked choice.

Two-path architecture (DK 2026-05-18 revision)

DK's directive: the surface "should allow student to test if we have it in the corpus, if not they must upload and we test if it is in the topic set or close enough." Two paths follow naturally. Path A — paper is in the corpus. The system already has the paper's fingerprint, Toulmin warrants, theory tags, IV/DV extraction, and any sidecars. The admit-mode evaluation runs against those existing records and returns immediately. The Ulrich (1984) worked example below shows this path. Path B — paper is not in the corpus. The student uploads the PDF. The system runs a four-step ingest-decision pipeline. First, the adaptive paper-type classifier (atlas_shared.AdaptiveClassifierSubsystem per the reference-paths memory) classifies the paper. Second, an embedding-based similarity check identifies the five corpus papers closest to it; if the maximum similarity falls below a threshold the paper is rejected as out-of-scope, with a brief explanation. Third, if the similarity passes threshold the paper is mapped to the closest matching corpus topic; if no topic is close enough the paper is offered to the student as a candidate new topic seed rather than admitted to an existing one. Fourth, if a corpus topic match exists, a lightweight version of the V7 extraction pipeline (CW proposes calling it V7-Lite) runs the minimal subset of stages needed for admit-mode: fingerprint extraction, IV/DV identification, methods extraction, and a VR-suitability mapping of the DV against the measurability page. The full V7 pipeline — visual cropping, science-writer summary, full PNU extraction, Toulmin warrant decomposition — is queued asynchronously rather than blocking the student's evaluation. The student's evaluation is returned within minutes; the full ingest lands hours later and updates the article-viewer page.

Path B — special process specification

DK's directive: "This process would have to be specified — eg VOI, related articles, how close to existing articles etc., its IV and DV and whether they are VR suitable already or we need to propose different measures." The V7-Lite pipeline's output is therefore a structured record with the following fields, each of which becomes a row of the student-facing evaluation. Topic-fit. The corpus topic to which the paper is closest, with a numerical similarity (cosine over embedding) and a list of the three nearest corpus papers. If similarity falls below the admission threshold, the field reports the rejection reason and ends. Related articles. The five corpus papers most similar to the uploaded paper, ranked. Each is linkable to the article viewer. This serves the student's question "is the work I'm proposing already substantially done?". VOI on this topic, conditional on the uploaded paper. The marginal contribution the uploaded paper would make were it brought into the corpus. Drawn from the VOI panel's operationalisation (see Surface 5) — high if the paper addresses an understudied intersection, low if it duplicates already-saturated coverage. IV. Extracted operationalisation of the independent variable. Reported in the paper's own terms, with a flag if the IV is confounded with another known construct (e.g., daylight intensity confounded with thermal load). DV. Extracted operationalisation of the dependent variable. Mapped against the VR-measurability page's positive and negative taxonomies (Surface 6, ka_vr_measurability_content_2026-05-18.md). The system reports one of: VR-tractable as-is (DV is on the positive list); substitution available (DV is on the negative list but a defensible substitute exists in the corpus); not VR-tractable (DV is on the negative list with no available substitute). Recommendation. A summary line — admit, substitute, or reject — with the rationale.

Annotations

The two-path branching (Path A in-corpus, Path B uploaded-with-V7-Lite-ingest) is the single most important addition from DK's 2026-05-18 review. The previous draft assumed every paper a student wants to evaluate already exists in the corpus — an unsafe assumption given that students bring papers from outside readings, coffee-shop discoveries, and the broader literature their professors recommend. Path B closes the gap.
The V7-Lite pipeline is a new piece of infrastructure. CW proposes it run the minimum subset of stages needed for the admit-mode evaluation (fingerprint, IV/DV extraction, methods extraction, VR-suitability mapping) while queuing the full V7 pipeline (visual cropping, science-writer summary, full PNU extraction, Toulmin warrants) for asynchronous completion. The student's evaluation returns within minutes; the full ingest lands hours later and incrementally upgrades the article-viewer page for the uploaded paper.
The rejection card (illustrative) is the third outcome the path B pipeline can produce. The substantive design choice: rejection must be explanatory rather than terse. A student who gets a rejection should understand why their paper falls outside the corpus and what they could do instead — pick from related corpus topics, or propose their paper as a new-topic seed for instructor review.
The substitution panel is the most consequential single piece of the Week-1 journey. It does real methodological reasoning: identify the underlying construct, find VR-tractable measures of that construct, surface the related papers, flag the project-feasibility implications.
The output is not "no" or "yes" — it is a structured evaluation that opens the related topic page if a substitute path exists. Even when the original paper is non-VR-tractable (Ulrich here), the path to a VR experiment is preserved through the analogues.

Dependencies

The substitution agent itself does not yet exist. Building it requires (a) the instrument registry to carry construct-operationalisation information uniformly across the corpus; (b) a ranking heuristic over candidate substitutes; (c) an explanation generator that produces the prose evaluation. This is a substantial Codex prompt and should be the most urgent of the new builds.
VR-tractability tags per measure require the VR-measurability content (Surface 6) to exist in machine-readable form, not just as a static explainer page. The quick-reference table in 160sp/ka_vr_measurability_content_2026-05-18.md includes short codes (f1.head_pose, f2.iat, f4.eda ...) for that purpose; a JSON sidecar should be derived from the table.
Project-feasibility assessment ("7-to-10-week feasibility") requires a small rule set: clinical population → no, multi-day exposure → no, fMRI → no, lab analogue with class-internal sample → yes. This rule set should be codified once and applied uniformly.
V7-Lite ingest pipeline (Path B, new). Codex owns the V7 pipeline. CW proposes V7-Lite as a subset: stages for (i) paper-type classification via atlas_shared.AdaptiveClassifierSubsystem, (ii) topic-fit embedding similarity, (iii) IV/DV extraction, (iv) methods extraction, (v) VR-suitability mapping against the measurability page short codes. The full V7 (visual cropping, science-writer summary, full PNU extraction, Toulmin warrants) runs asynchronously after the student's evaluation returns. Codex prompt to be drafted in a follow-up sprint.
Topic-similarity threshold. The 0.55 cosine threshold shown in the Path B mock is illustrative and needs calibration. A reasonable calibration protocol: hold out ten in-corpus papers per topic, compute their cosine similarity to the rest of the corpus, set the threshold so that 95% of held-out papers admit to their correct topic. Threshold becomes a per-topic parameter rather than a single global value.
New-topic-seed path. When the uploaded paper does not match any existing topic but is otherwise plausible, the system offers it as a new-topic seed for instructor review. This is a flag-for-instructor flow rather than an auto-admit; design the queue interface and notification path.
VOI conditional on uploaded paper. The VOI computation for Path B is conditional VOI ("what would this paper add given the existing corpus?") rather than topic-level VOI ("how saturated is this topic?"). Both are needed and the panel should clarify which the VOI surface (Surface 5) should produce in each context.

Decisions

Should the surface accept a free-text paper title or only a DOI? CW recommends both, but DOI takes priority — the system can look up the structured record. Free text becomes a search.
What does the surface do when the paper is outside the Atlas corpus? Two options: (a) refuse to evaluate, telling the student to bring a corpus paper; (b) accept and run a best-effort evaluation against generic VR-measurability rules. CW recommends (a) for Week 1 (avoid over-promising), but the system should suggest related corpus papers.

Surface 4b · Choose a measure for my VR experiment (choice-mode · Week 3 hand-off)

Reached from Surface 4 after admit-mode succeeds, or from Surface 3 at Week-3 transition · file: ka_choose_measure_for_vr.html (new)

What this surface is for

A student has settled on a topic in Weeks 1–2; the topic survived admit-mode (Surface 4) because at least one VR-tractable measure exists. By Week 3 the student must commit to a stimulus and a measurement protocol, and the typical situation is that multiple admissible measures are on the table, each with a profile of psychometric strengths and weaknesses that only surface when the candidates are laid out side by side. This surface is the side-by-side comparison. Surface 7 · Methodological pitfalls — beyond Cook & Campbell

Mock layout

Methodological pitfalls a 7-to-10-week project can still respect
Even a short course project should know the standards it cannot meet, and should meet the ones it can. This page covers the threats beyond Cook & Campbell's classic four validities, drawn from the post-2010 methodological-reform literature.

Threats your project should anticipate

  • Cook & Campbell's four validities (internal, external, construct, statistical conclusion) — the classic framework. Sections of the source document.
  • Garden of forking paths (Gelman & Loken 2014) — every analytic decision you make after seeing data inflates false positives. Mitigation: pre-specify your analytic plan.
  • HARKing (Kerr 1998) — hypothesising after results are known. Mitigation: write down your hypothesis before you run the experiment.
  • P-hacking (Simmons, Nelson, Simonsohn 2011) — undisclosed flexibility. Mitigation: report all measures, all conditions, all exclusions.
  • WEIRD-sample issues (Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan 2010) — your UCSD sample is WEIRD. State this in your discussion; do not over-generalise.
  • Multiple comparisons — if you run twenty tests, you expect one significant by chance. Mitigation: pre-specify primary hypothesis(es); correct or treat the rest as exploratory.

Standards a 7-to-10-week project can meet

  • Pre-registration. Free, takes an hour, prevents the garden of forking paths. Use OSF AsPredicted.
  • Random assignment. Required for causal inference. Use a coin flip, a script, or the experiment's randomiser.
  • Pre-specified primary outcome. One measure, one hypothesis, written before data collection.
  • Pre-specified analysis plan. Include in your pre-registration. Departures from it should be flagged in the write-up.
  • Honest sample reporting. Who, how many, exclusions, demographics, dropouts.
  • Effect-size reporting with CI. Not just p-values. Cohen's d for two-group; partial η² for ANOVA.
  • Open materials and data. Deposit your A-Frame scenes, your analysis script, your deidentified data on OSF.
For the curious: the full COGS 160 Fall design guide (linked) covers all of this in depth, with examples and references. This page is the operational summary.

Annotations

The page distinguishes threats to anticipate (you may not be able to eliminate, but you should know they exist) from standards to meet (you can and should). For a 7-to-10-week project, hitting the second list is realistic; acknowledging the first is methodological honesty.

Surface 7b · Cook & Campbell's four validities — showcase

Reached from Surface 1, from Surface 7, and from the "Why is this contested?" CTA on contested DYK cards · file: ka_validities_showcase.html (new) · added per DK 2026-05-18: "You must also showcase Cook & Campbell's classic four validities"

What this surface is for

A standalone showcase of the four-validities taxonomy that descends from Campbell & Stanley (1963), reached its canonical statement in Cook & Campbell (1979), and is updated in Shadish, Cook & Campbell (2002). The taxonomy organises the threats a study faces into four categories — internal, external, construct, and statistical conclusion validity — and is the standard frame Cognitive Science methodology courses use. The surface is intentionally separated from Surface 7 (the broader methodological-pitfalls explainer) because Cook & Campbell's framework deserves direct exposure rather than being subsumed; the broader page covers replication-crisis pitfalls, construct-proliferation, WEIRD-bias, demand characteristics, and VR-specific issues that Cook & Campbell do not cover (because they predate them).

Mock layout

Cook & Campbell's four validities
Every experimental claim can be challenged on four independent grounds. The taxonomy below is the canonical organisation. On each card, the threats are the standard list in Shadish, Cook & Campbell (2002), §2; the bold prose explains what the validity asks of your study.
1 · Internal validity
Did the manipulation cause the change in the outcome, within this study?
Classic threats:
  • History. An event other than the manipulation co-occurs and may have caused the effect.
  • Maturation. Participants change over time independently of the manipulation.
  • Testing. The pre-test itself changes the post-test score.
  • Instrumentation. The measure changes between pre and post (different rater, different calibration).
  • Statistical regression. Extreme baseline scores naturally regress toward the mean.
  • Selection. Groups differed before the manipulation began.
  • Attrition. Participants drop out differentially between conditions.
  • Ambiguous temporal precedence. Unclear which variable changed first.
For your VR project: random assignment, blinding to condition, equating exposure duration, and adequate within-Ss baselines address most of these.
2 · External validity
Does the effect generalise — to other people, other settings, other times?
Classic threats:
  • Interaction of selection with treatment. The effect works only for the kind of person you sampled.
  • Interaction of setting with treatment. The effect works only in your lab or your VR.
  • Interaction of history with treatment. The effect works only at this point in time.
For your VR project: report your sample's demographic composition and acknowledge VR-specific scope; do not over-claim transfer to the real world unless your design tests it (X4 in the VR-measurability page).
3 · Construct validity
Are your independent and dependent variables actually measuring what you say they are?
Classic threats:
  • Inadequate explication of constructs. The construct is too vague to operationalise unambiguously.
  • Mono-operation bias. Only one operationalisation of the IV — others might not produce the effect.
  • Mono-method bias. Only one measurement method for the DV — biases shared across methods inflate the result.
  • Confounding constructs with levels. Different levels of the IV co-vary with different constructs.
  • Experimenter expectancies. The experimenter unconsciously cues participants.
  • Novelty and disruption effects. The participant responds to the novelty of the situation, not the manipulation.
For your VR project: pair a behavioural measure (F2) with a self-report measure (F3); if they converge, the construct claim is stronger.
4 · Statistical conclusion validity
Given your data, is the inference to "there is an effect" statistically warranted?
Classic threats:
  • Low statistical power. Too few participants to detect the effect you are after.
  • Violated assumptions of statistical tests. Normality, independence, equal variances.
  • Fishing and the error rate problem. Multiple comparisons inflate false-positive rates.
  • Unreliability of measures. Noisy measurement attenuates the effect estimate.
  • Restriction of range. Truncated IV or DV reduces detectable variance.
  • Unreliability of treatment implementation. The manipulation was not delivered the same way every time.
For your VR project: do a power calculation in Week 2 (Cohen 1988 standard tables suffice for a class project); pre-specify your statistical test; report effect sizes with confidence intervals, not just p-values.
Worked example — why the ceiling-height finding is contested
The Meyers-Levy & Zhu (2007) finding that ≥3.0 m ceilings bias toward divergent thinking is a Construct-validity-and-Internal-validity puzzle. The construct-validity challenge: the manipulation (high vs low ceiling photographs) co-varies with several constructs — perceived spaciousness, lighting variation, the visible-vegetation fraction in some images. The reported divergent-thinking effect could index any of them, not specifically "ceiling height." The internal-validity challenge in the replications: the failed replication studies used different image sets, different presentation durations, and different participant populations. Whether the failures are evidence against the effect or against the operationalisation is itself contested. What you would have to do to settle it: a within-subjects design that varies ceiling height holding other room properties constant, with an independent measure of perceived spaciousness as a covariate. This is a tractable Week-1 topic if you accept that you are testing a more carefully specified version of the original claim.

Exemplary VR experiments
Eight well-designed VR experiments, with explanations of what makes each one a good role model. Read these to calibrate your own design intuitions.
Riva et al. (2007) — Affective space in immersive VR
Within-Ss design, N = 36; manipulated affective qualities of the same architectural space (warm/cool, open/closed) while measuring mood and presence. Why it's a role model: single-variable manipulation, well-validated mood measure (PANAS), clean within-Ss controls.
Slater et al. (2010) — Body-ownership illusion in VR
Between-Ss design, N = 40; manipulated the perspective from which participants saw a virtual body and measured ownership ratings. Why it's a role model: falsifiable prediction, theory-driven hypothesis, multiple converging measures (self-report + behavioural).
Smaldino-style example — small-N pre-registered VR study
Pre-registered, N = 48, single primary outcome, predicted direction stated in advance. Why it's a role model: the student-relevant features — small N, single outcome, pre-registration, honest reporting — are exactly what a 7-to-10-week project can match.
Six more exemplars in production. Each will carry: the design, the construct, the measure, and the one or two features that make it a role model for a 7-to-10-week VR project.
