The Transferable Skill

By Week 5, you will be able to sit down with two or three AI systems and, in a single afternoon, produce a working component that meets a defined specification. We call this "The Afternoon with Three AIs" — and it is the skill that every employer, graduate program, and research lab will value in you.

The workflow: Panel → Spec → Contracts → Implement → Verify → Iterate. Every step has specific techniques. This course teaches them all, using a real system as the practice substrate.

The Practice Substrate: Knowledge_ATLAS

Knowledge_ATLAS is a large knowledge system that maps what the scientific literature says about how built environments affect human cognition, emotion, and behavior. It contains a Web of Belief with 5,461 interconnected claims, drawn from 800+ scientific papers, organized into 25 research fronts. It has a Bayesian causal bridge that can predict what will happen if you change a building. It has an extraction pipeline that scores 91/100 on the AESHI quality audit.

It also has six major capability gaps — things it should be able to do but can't yet. Your job is to close those gaps. Each student track addresses one or more gaps. By the end of the quarter, KA will be measurably more capable than it is today, and you will have the methodology to build systems like it in any domain.

The Method: AI-Directed System Building

This is not a "prompt engineering" course. This is a course in directing AI systems to build verified knowledge artifacts. The distinction matters: prompt engineering is about getting better chatbot answers; AI-directed system building is about producing components that meet formal specifications, pass defined tests, and integrate correctly with a larger system.

The Seven-Step Workflow

1

Define the Problem

What exactly should this component do? What questions must it answer? Who is the user? Start with use cases, not features. "A researcher asks: what is the strongest evidence for biophilic design effects on stress?" is a better problem definition than "build an evidence browser."

2

Convene an Expert Panel

Don't guess what the system should do — ask experts. Direct an AI to simulate 3–5 domain experts, each grounded in named researchers and their published positions. Include at least one adversarial voice. Include a methodologist. The panel argues about what matters and what "good" looks like.

3

Produce a Spec with Success Conditions

Convert the panel's output into a contract: inputs, outputs, invariants, and measurable success conditions. "Precision > 0.85 on a 50-paper test set" is a success condition. "It should work well" is not. You cannot verify what you cannot measure.

4

Force Planning Before Implementation

Direct the AI to produce a strategy document: what approach, what architecture, what risks, what testing plan. The prompt says: "Do NOT write any code. Produce a strategy document." This prevents the most common AI failure: producing 400 lines of professional-looking code that solves the wrong problem.

5

Implement with Parallel AIs

Independent components go to different AIs simultaneously. One builds the keyword scorer, another builds the journal quality scorer. Division of labor with clear interfaces. Each AI receives the spec and the vocabulary — not just a vague instruction.

6

Verify Against the Spec

Run the output against the success conditions. Compare the AI's self-assessment ("this should work well!") with the actual numbers. The AI that builds should not be the sole evaluator of its own work. Use cross-AI verification, automated tests, and human spot-checks.

7

Iterate with Ruthless Prompts

Where output fails the spec, write a ruthless correction: show the specific error, explain why it's wrong, demand a specific fix, forbid the pattern that caused the error. Ruthlessness is for execution, not exploration. It is precise, not angry.

Why This Works

What looks like "an afternoon's work with three AIs" is the compressed output of years of hard-won craft. Students who skip the panel step produce systems that solve the wrong problem. Students who skip the spec step produce systems that cannot be tested. Students who skip verification produce systems that look impressive and are subtly wrong. The methodology exists because every shortcut has been tried and has failed.

The Five AI Failure Modes

AIs fail in predictable ways. Knowing these patterns lets you prevent them:

1. Plausible Hallucination

The AI generates content that reads correctly, uses domain vocabulary, and is completely made up. Fake citations, invented effect sizes, fabricated study descriptions.

2. Wrong-Problem Drift

The AI starts on task X and gradually drifts to task Y because Y is easier. Common in multi-step prompts where attention degrades.

3. Self-Congratulatory Report

The AI implements, tests its own work, and reports success. The tests test what the code does, not what it should do.

4. Consensus Fabrication

When synthesizing multiple perspectives, the AI smooths over disagreements. This destroys exactly the information that matters most.

5. Premature Abstraction

The AI produces abstract, general output when concrete, specific output was needed. "Several studies found..." instead of "3 studies (Mehta 2012, N=45; Steidle 2013, N=80) found..."

Knowledge_ATLAS

Knowledge_ATLAS (Architecture for Typed, Layered Assessment of Science) is a computational knowledge system that maps what science knows about how built environments affect human cognition, emotion, and behavior. It is the practice substrate for this course — the system you will improve.

What It Contains

Web of Belief

5,461 interconnected beliefs organized as a directed multigraph. Each belief has a credence (0–1), warrant type, and connections to supporting evidence.

Extraction Pipeline

800+ scientific papers processed through a seven-stage pipeline: PDF ingestion, metadata extraction, article typing, table detection, claim extraction, variable resolution, web integration. AESHI quality score: 91/100.

Bayesian Causal Bridge

A structural causal model that translates knowledge ("what we believe") into prediction ("what will happen if we intervene"). Uses the projection calculus with four factors: warrant type, warrant strength, population transfer, and lab effect size.

25 Research Fronts

The corpus is organized into 25 active research areas, from "Ceiling Height and Creativity" to "Biophilic Design and Stress Recovery." Each front has structured evidence summaries, findings tables, and meta-reviews.

The Three-Layer Architecture

ATLAS separates what we know from what we can predict:

EN

Epistemic Network

A labeled directed multigraph representing our state of knowledge. Contains multiple evidence types, latent intermediates, theoretical claims, parallel edges, and cycles. Seven warrant types: CONSTITUTIVE, MECHANISM, EMPIRICAL_ASSOCIATION, THEORY_DERIVED, FUNCTIONAL, ANALOGICAL, CAPACITY.

π

Projection Function

Maps from EN to BN. Translates knowledge into prediction using the four-factor formula: logit(p_target) = d(τ) · ω · δ · logit(p_lab). Each factor captures a distinct epistemic dimension.

BN

Bayesian Network

A directed acyclic graph with conditional probability tables. The operational prediction engine: "What will happen if we intervene on X?" Uses Pearl's do-calculus for causal reasoning.

What It Can't Do Yet (The Six Gaps)

These are the questions KA should answer but currently can't. Each gap is a concrete failure that your track work will fix:

Gap 1: "I have an EmotiBit and a VR headset. What's the highest-value experiment I can run?" → KA doesn't know what equipment you have or how to condition its recommendations on your resources.

Gap 2: "Does natural light improve performance, and through what mechanism?" → KA has the evidence but can't route a natural-language question to the right causal chain and present the full mechanism pathway.

Gap 3: "Show me everything about lighting → circadian rhythm and let me follow the paths." → KA has the graph structure but no interface lets you explore it visually, following edges from cause to mechanism to outcome.

Gap 4: "Should I put plants in this hospital lobby?" → A practitioner needs a plain-language answer with honest uncertainty, not a research paper. KA can't simplify its knowledge for non-expert users.

Gap 5: "If someone proved that fractal patterns reduce cortical prediction error, how would that change KA's beliefs?" → KA can't run hypothetical scenarios and propagate their consequences through the web.

Gap 6: A single question might need evidence from multiple fronts, theory from multiple frameworks, and predictions under multiple scenarios. KA can't decompose complex queries automatically.

Prompt Design

This course teaches prompt design as a component of a larger methodology: using AI as a directed instrument in the construction of verified knowledge artifacts. The goal is not "getting good output" but producing output that meets a formal specification, passes defined tests, and integrates correctly with a larger system.

The Five Components of a Production Prompt

Component 1

Role Assignment

Who is the AI pretending to be? Specific enough to activate domain knowledge. "A bibliometrician who specializes in systematic review methodology" — not "a helpful assistant."

Component 2

Task Definition

What exactly should the AI produce? Defined in terms of output format, not just intent. JSON schema, not prose. Testable, not aspirational.

Component 3

Constraints and Prohibitions

The "DO NOT" pattern. AIs have systematic failure modes; constraints prevent them. "DO NOT invent citations. DO NOT summarize when asked to extract. DO NOT evaluate your own output."

Component 4

Context Loading

Vocabulary sheets, canonical term lists, reference documents. The AI doesn't know your domain vocabulary — teach it explicitly, or it will map "illuminance" to "enlightenment."

Component 5

Output Format Specification

The exact structure of the response. JSON schema with required fields, not free text. The format is the contract between the AI's output and the next step in your pipeline.

Prompt Design Cheat Sheet

Before You Prompt

  • Do I have a contract with success conditions?
  • Do I have a test set to verify against?
  • Do I have a vocabulary sheet for my domain?
  • Do I know which failure modes to watch for?
  • Have I chosen the right model for this task?

The Prompt Itself

  • Role assignment (specific, not generic)
  • Task definition (output format, not just intent)
  • Constraints and DO NOTs (at least 3)
  • Context loading (vocabulary, examples, reference docs)
  • Output format specification (schema, not prose)

After You Get Output

  • Does it meet the contract? (Check every field)
  • Does it pass the success conditions? (Run the tests)
  • Is it suspiciously good? (Check for hallucination)
  • Did the AI evaluate its own work? (Don't trust self-assessment)
  • Would a different AI produce different output? (Cross-verify)

When It Fails

  • Identify the failure mode (which of the 5?)
  • Write a ruthless prompt (show error, explain why, demand fix, forbid pattern)
  • Re-run and verify improvement
  • Add the failure to your constraint set for next time
  • If it fails 3 times, restructure the prompt or switch approach
